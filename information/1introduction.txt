Precise orbit determination of natural satellites is fundamental to studies of planetary system dynamics and long-term ephemeris prediction. Optical astrometric observations provide the primary positional constraints for improving satellite orbits. However, such observations typically span long time intervals and originate from multiple observatories, instruments, and reduction schemes. The consistency of these heterogeneous datasets directly impacts the accuracy and stability of the resulting orbital solutions.

A large body of natural satellite astrometry has been compiled in the Natural Satellite Data Center (NSDC) maintained by IMCCE. Owing to the diversity of data sources and the long temporal coverage, inconsistencies related to the use of different reference star catalogs have become increasingly evident when these observations are combined for orbit determination. Differences in reference frames, regional systematic errors, and proper motion accuracy among catalogs introduce systematic biases in the reduced positions, which may accumulate over time and degrade the overall consistency of orbital solutions.

Since 1986, we have conducted continuous observations of natural satellites in the Jovian, Saturnian, Uranian, and Neptunian systems, resulting in approximately 51,170 astrometric measurements incorporated into the NSDC database. Based on these data, a series of studies has been carried out, including observations and orbit improvements for the Saturnian system and Phoebe, orbit determination of Triton, and re-reduction of historical observations within the Gaia reference frame \citep{ShenQiao1995,Qiao1999,Qiao2004,Shen2005,Qiao2007,Qiao2008,Qiao2014,Zhang2014,Yan2019Triton,Yan2020Triton,Yan2023Saturn}. These efforts demonstrate that reprocessing historical observations within a uniform high-precision reference frame is an effective approach to improving data consistency.

Systematic errors in star catalogs have been investigated extensively. \citet{Chesley2010} identified regional systematic biases through residual analysis of asteroid astrometry and introduced a spatially resolved correction scheme. \citet{Farnocchia2015} extended this approach by incorporating proper motion corrections. More recently, \citet{Eggl2020} utilized the high-precision Gaia reference frame to derive consistent position and proper motion corrections for a wide range of historical catalogs. These studies establish that catalog debiasing within a common reference frame is essential for improving the consistency of astrometric data.

In the context of natural satellites, similar approaches have begun to be explored. For example, \citet{Yuan2023} performed a uniform reanalysis of Neptunian satellite observations based on the Gaia DR2 reference frame and demonstrated the applicability of catalog debiasing methods to satellite astrometry. In addition, our group has conducted Gaia-based re-reductions of selected historical observations, confirming the effectiveness of this approach in improving observational consistency. Nevertheless, a systematic and unified debiasing of commonly used reference catalogs in natural satellite astrometry is still lacking.

Following this approach, we implement a catalog debiasing scheme based on Gaia DR3 as a reference. In this work, we adopt Gaia DR3 as a common reference frame and derive systematic corrections for 17 star catalogs frequently used in historical observations of natural satellites. Building upon established catalog debiasing schemes, we introduce adaptations tailored to the characteristics of satellite astrometry. The resulting correction framework provides a consistent basis for reprocessing historical observations and supports improved orbit determination, tidal evolution studies, and investigations of complex dynamical behavior in satellite systems.
