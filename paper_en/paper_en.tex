\begin{abstract}

Precise orbit determination of natural satellites relies on the consistent combination of heterogeneous astrometric observations spanning long time intervals, but the use of different reference star catalogs introduces spatially correlated systematic biases that degrade data consistency and limit orbit accuracy. In this work, we construct a unified correction framework based on the Gaia DR3 reference frame and derive position and proper motion corrections for 17 star catalogs commonly used in natural satellite astrometry. The method employs rigorous epoch propagation, HEALPix-based spatial partitioning, and robust median statistics to estimate local systematics, and reconstructs a continuous correction field using radial basis function interpolation to achieve high-resolution corrections while preserving statistical stability. The derived corrections exhibit a clear hierarchy among catalogs, with modern catalogs showing milliarcsecond-level systematics, whereas older photographic catalogs display position offsets exceeding $10^2$~mas and significant spatial structure. This framework provides a consistent basis for reprocessing historical observations and supports improved orbit determination and dynamical studies of natural satellite systems.

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction} \label{sec:intro}

Precise orbit determination of natural satellites is fundamental to studies of planetary system dynamics and long-term ephemeris prediction. Optical astrometric observations provide the primary positional constraints for improving satellite orbits. However, such observations typically span long time intervals and originate from multiple observatories, instruments, and reduction schemes. The consistency of these heterogeneous datasets directly impacts the accuracy and stability of the resulting orbital solutions and their uncertainty characterization \citep{Desmars2013}.

A large body of natural satellite astrometry has been compiled in the Natural Satellite Data Center (NSDC) maintained by IMCCE. Owing to the diversity of data sources and the long temporal coverage, inconsistencies related to the use of different reference star catalogs have become increasingly evident when these observations are combined for orbit determination. Differences in reference frames, regional systematic errors, and proper motion accuracy among catalogs introduce systematic biases in the reduced positions, which can accumulate over time and degrade the overall consistency of orbital solutions.

Since 1986, we have conducted continuous observations of natural satellites in the Jovian, Saturnian, Uranian, and Neptunian systems, resulting in approximately 51,170 astrometric measurements that have been incorporated into the NSDC database. Based on these data, a series of studies has been carried out on the astrometry and orbit improvement of Saturnian satellites, the major Uranian satellites, and the irregular satellite Phoebe, as well as on the digitization and reduction of historical natural-satellite plates \citep{Qiao1999,Shen2002,Qiao2006Phoebe,Yan2016Plates}. These studies demonstrate that reprocessing historical observations within a uniform high-precision reference frame is an effective approach to improving data consistency.

Systematic errors in star catalogs have been investigated extensively. Early analyses of asteroid astrometry showed that optical observations contain non-negligible systematic components \citep{Carpino2003}. \citet{Chesley2010} identified regional systematic biases through residual analysis and introduced a spatially resolved correction scheme using 2MASS as the reference catalog \citep{Skrutskie2006}. \citet{Tholen2013b} further showed that the lack of proper motion information in 2MASS can generate regional signatures in high-precision astrometry, motivating the proper-motion-aware treatment developed by \citet{Farnocchia2015} with a PPMXL-based reference \citep{Roeser2010}. More generally, the reliability of astrometric catalogs and their proper motions must be assessed with care in precision applications \citep{Teixeira2014}. More recently, \citet{Eggl2020} utilized the high-precision Gaia reference frame to derive consistent position and proper motion corrections for a wide range of historical catalogs. Taken together, these studies establish that catalog debiasing within a common reference frame is essential for improving the consistency of astrometric data.

In the context of natural satellites, similar approaches have only recently begun to be explored. For example, \citet{Yuan2021} performed a uniform reanalysis of Neptunian satellite observations based on the Gaia DR2 reference frame and demonstrated the applicability of catalog debiasing methods to satellite astrometry. In addition, our group has conducted Gaia-based re-reductions of selected historical observations, confirming the effectiveness of this approach in improving observational consistency. Nevertheless, a systematic and unified debiasing framework for commonly used reference catalogs in natural satellite astrometry is still lacking.

In this work, we adopt Gaia DR3 as the common reference frame and derive systematic corrections for 17 star catalogs frequently used in historical observations of natural satellites. Building upon established catalog debiasing schemes, we introduce adaptations tailored to satellite astrometry, including refined cross-matching criteria and additional validation steps. The resulting correction framework provides a consistent basis for reprocessing historical observations and supports improved orbit determination, tidal evolution studies, and investigations of complex dynamical behavior in satellite systems.

\section{Reference and Astrometric Catalogs} \label{sec:catalogs}

\subsection{Gaia Catalog Series} \label{subsec:gaia}

Since 2013, the Gaia mission has conducted all-sky high-precision astrometric observations, providing measurements for approximately 1.8 billion sources. The first data release (Gaia DR1) provided a limited five-parameter astrometric solution only for the TGAS subset, while the majority of sources lacked proper motion information \citep{GaiaDR1,Lindegren2016}. As such, DR1 represents a transitional catalog and is not suitable for high-precision astrometric calibration.

Gaia DR2 marked the first large-scale release of a homogeneous five-parameter astrometric solution, significantly improving the consistency of the reference frame and establishing a foundation for systematic error analysis of external star catalogs and Solar System astrometry \citep{GaiaDR2}.

Gaia EDR3 and DR3 maintain a consistent astrometric parameterization, with the DR3 astrometric solution based on EDR3, while benefiting from an extended observational time baseline of approximately 34 months and improved modeling of spacecraft attitude, chromatic effects, and instrument calibration \citep{GaiaDR3,Lindegren2021EDR3}. Compared to DR2, DR3 achieves a reduction of about 20\% in parallax formal uncertainties and more than a factor of two improvement in proper motion precision \citep{CantatGaudin2021}. In addition, the stability of the reference frame and the characterization of systematic errors are further improved \citep{Fabricius2021,Lindegren2021Systematics}. For these reasons, Gaia DR3 is adopted as the reference catalog in this work.

\citet{Eggl2020} demonstrated that systematic biases in historical star catalogs can be effectively characterized through regional comparisons with a high-precision reference catalog, and established a catalog debiasing framework based on Gaia DR2. Building on this approach, the present work extends the debiasing scheme to natural satellite astrometry within the Gaia reference system. By employing Gaia DR3 as a more precise and stable reference, the systematic differences between catalogs can be more reliably quantified, enabling a consistent correction of historical observations.

\subsection{Historical Astrometric Catalogs} \label{subsec:historical}

In contrast to \citet{Eggl2020}, which focuses on star catalogs used in asteroid astrometry, this work targets catalogs that are frequently employed in natural satellite observations, including additional historical catalogs relevant to this context. This is particularly true for older satellite reductions based on Guide Star Catalog releases \citep{Lasker1999,Morrison2001GSC12}.

Optical astrometric observations from different epochs rely on a variety of reference star catalogs that differ significantly in epoch, sky coverage, source density, and available astrometric parameters. The catalogs considered in this study are selected based on their prevalence in historical observations of natural satellites and their representation of major astrometric reference systems.

These catalogs can be broadly classified into three categories. Early photographic catalogs, such as USNO-A and GSC, generally lack proper motion information and can introduce time-dependent positional errors \citep{Monet1998USNOA2,Morrison2001GSC12}. Intermediate catalogs, including PPM, ACT, and the UCAC series, provide improved astrometric information but may still exhibit regional systematic biases \citep{Urban1998ACT,Zacharias2004UCAC2,Zacharias2013UCAC4}. High-precision catalogs such as Hipparcos and Tycho-2 offer a stable reference frame but are limited by relatively low source density \citep{ESA1997Hipparcos,Hog2000Tycho2}. These characteristics motivate the need for catalog debiasing within a unified reference frame. A summary of the catalogs adopted in this work is provided in Table~\ref{tab:catalog_overview}.

\section{Basic Framework of the Correction Method} \label{sec:methods}

\subsection{Overview of the Method}

The overall procedure consists of five main steps: (1) transform all catalogs to a common reference frame and comparison epoch; (2) partition the celestial sphere using a HEALPix tessellation; (3) perform cross-matching between the target catalog and Gaia DR3 within each sky region; (4) estimate local systematic offsets in position and proper motion; and (5) construct a correction model and generate tabulated corrections.

The method uses a target star catalog and Gaia DR3 as input and produces HEALPix-based correction parameters, including positional offsets $(\Delta \alpha_{*,2000}, \Delta \delta_{2000})$ and proper motion biases $(\Delta \mu_{\alpha *}, \Delta \mu_{\delta})$, where $\alpha_* \equiv \alpha \cos\delta$. All correction quantities are defined at the comparison epoch J2000.0.

\subsection{Epoch Propagation and Frame Transformation}

To ensure consistency, all catalogs are transformed to a common reference frame and comparison epoch. J2000.0 is adopted as the comparison epoch since it is the reference epoch for most historical catalogs considered in this work.

For catalogs already in the ICRS, positions are propagated to epoch J2000.0 when proper motion information is available. For catalogs lacking proper motions, Gaia positions are propagated to the catalog epoch, and the comparison is performed at that epoch. The resulting positional differences derived at the catalog epoch are subsequently propagated to J2000.0 using Gaia proper motions to ensure consistency of the correction parameters.

For catalogs not originally in the ICRS (e.g., FK4), coordinates are first transformed into the ICRS, and matching is carried out at the corresponding comparison epochs \citep{Murray1989,Ma1998ICRF,Fey2015}. This procedure ensures that the measured differences primarily reflect intrinsic catalog systematics and avoids introducing additional uncertainties from inaccurate proper motions in historical catalogs.

Gaia DR3 adopts a reference epoch of J2016.0. Source positions are propagated to the desired comparison epoch using the published astrometric parameters (positions, proper motions, and parallaxes). Radial velocities are included when available and assumed to be zero otherwise, which introduces negligible errors at the precision level considered here.

\subsection{Sky Partitioning and Cross-Matching}

After the reference frame and epoch are unified, the celestial sphere is divided using a HEALPix \citep{Gorski2005} tessellation with $N_{\rm side}=64$, corresponding to $N_{\rm pix}=49{,}152$ equal-area tiles, each covering approximately $0.839\ {\rm deg}^2$.

Cross-matching is performed within each tile, and all angular distances are computed on the celestial sphere. For each source in the target catalog, Gaia DR3 candidates are searched within a search radius of $1''$. The matching criteria are defined as follows:

(1) If a single candidate is found, the match is accepted.

(2) If multiple candidates are present, the nearest neighbor is accepted only if $d_1 < 0.2\, d_2$, where $d_1$ and $d_2$ are the angular distances to the nearest and second-nearest neighbors, respectively.

(3) If no candidate is found, the source is rejected.

All matches are performed using coordinates expressed in the same reference frame and epoch.

\subsection{Bias Estimation}

For each matched pair, the differences in position and proper motion are defined as
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\Delta \alpha_* = (\alpha_{\rm cat}-\alpha_{\rm Gaia})\cos\delta,\quad
\Delta \delta = \delta_{\rm cat}-\delta_{\rm Gaia},\\
\Delta \mu_{\alpha *} = \mu_{\alpha *{\rm cat}}-\mu_{\alpha *{\rm Gaia}},\quad
\Delta \mu_{\delta} = \mu_{\delta{\rm cat}}-\mu_{\delta{\rm Gaia}}.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

For catalogs without proper motion information, only positional differences are used, while proper motion terms are constrained through Gaia-based propagation.

Within each HEALPix tile, the median of these differences is computed and referenced to epoch J2000.0, yielding the systematic correction parameters. Tiles with insufficient matched sources are excluded to ensure statistical reliability.
\begin{equation}
(\Delta \alpha_{*,2000}, \Delta \delta_{2000}, \Delta \mu_{\alpha *}, \Delta \mu_{\delta}).
\end{equation}

These quantities represent local catalog systematics referenced to epoch J2000.0. The use of median statistics provides robustness against outliers without the need for additional rejection criteria.

For an arbitrary epoch $t$, the corrections are given by a linear model:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\Delta \alpha_*(t) &= \Delta \alpha_{*,2000} + \Delta \mu_{\alpha *}\,\Delta t,\\
\Delta \delta(t) &= \Delta \delta_{2000} + \Delta \mu_{\delta}\,\Delta t,\\
\quad \Delta t &= t - 2000.0.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}


\subsection{Correction Tables and Application}

The resulting corrections are stored on a HEALPix grid. Each tile is associated with a set of parameters, including the HEALPix index, $\Delta \alpha_{2000}$, $\Delta \delta_{2000}$, $\Delta \mu_{\alpha *}$, and $\Delta \mu_{\delta}$.

In practical applications, the corresponding tile is identified from the sky position of an observation, and the associated parameters are used to compute $\Delta \alpha_*(t)$ and $\Delta \delta(t)$. The corrected positions are obtained by applying these offsets, thereby aligning the observations with the Gaia DR3 reference frame.

\subsection{Spatial Structure of the Systematic Biases} \label{subsec:spatial}

The correction fields exhibit clear spatial organization across the celestial sphere. As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:bias_usno_a2_2x2}, the USNO-A2.0 corrections display large-scale gradients together with localized discontinuities. Similar spatial patterns are observed in other historical catalogs, although their morphology and characteristic scales differ.

These structures demonstrate that the correction fields are dominated by spatially correlated systematics. In particular, the presence of coherent large-scale patterns indicates that the derived corrections trace intrinsic catalog biases rather than stochastic matching errors. Consequently, global summary statistics alone are insufficient to fully characterize the spatial behavior of catalog systematics.

\subsection{Origin of the Bias Patterns} \label{subsec:interpretation}

The morphology of the correction fields reflects the observational and reduction characteristics of the underlying catalogs. Three representative classes can be identified.

Photographic catalogs (e.g., GSC~1.2, AGK3, SAO, and Yale) show block-like or discontinuous structures associated with independent plate-based reductions\citep{Platais1998,Eichhorn1974}. This behavior can be quantified through the inter-pixel discontinuity

\begin{equation}
\delta_{\mathrm{disc}}(i,j)=|V(i)-V(j)|,
\end{equation}

where $i$ and $j$ denote adjacent HEALPix pixels and $V$ represents a correction component. For these catalogs, more than 97\% of neighboring regions exhibit discontinuities exceeding $100$~mas, and a substantial fraction reach the arcsecond level, indicating globally inconsistent plate-to-plate calibrations.

Catalogs based on scanning strategies (e.g., UCAC2) exhibit stripe-like patterns aligned with the scan direction\citep{Zacharias2017UCAC5}, with amplitudes enhanced in crowded regions. This behavior is largely mitigated in UCAC4, consistent with improved reduction procedures.

Catalogs lacking reliable proper motion information (e.g., USNO-A2.0) display smooth large-scale gradients in the propagated position corrections. These patterns arise from the accumulation of proper-motion mismatches over long temporal baselines\citep{Vasiliev2019}.

These results show that both the amplitude and morphology of the correction fields are controlled by the intrinsic properties of the source catalogs.

\subsection{Smoothing and Practical Implementation} \label{subsec:smoothing}

\begin{figure}[ht!]
\plotone{"../Figue/bias_ucac4_zoom_compare_n256.png"}
\caption{Example of RBF smoothing applied to the correction field.}
\label{fig:bias_ucac4_zoom_compare_n256}
\end{figure}

\begin{figure}[ht!]
\plotone{"../Figue/figure_Y_jump_distribution.png"}
\caption{Cumulative distributions of inter-pixel discontinuities before and after smoothing.}
\label{fig:bias_jump}
\end{figure}

For practical applications, a higher-resolution correction map is required to better represent the spatial structure of catalog systematics. However, directly estimating corrections on a finer grid would significantly reduce the number of matched stars per pixel, thereby degrading the statistical robustness of the median estimates. In this regime, small-scale variations are increasingly dominated by sampling noise rather than genuine systematic patterns.

To balance spatial resolution and statistical stability, we adopt a similar strategy to that of \citet{Eggl2020}. Instead of recomputing corrections on a finer grid, we reconstruct a continuous correction field from the statistically robust coarse-grid estimates using radial basis function (RBF) interpolation with a thin-plate spline kernel \citep{Wahba1990,Schaback1995}. The final correction product is then provided on a higher-resolution HEALPix grid with \texttt{nside}=256.

In practice, the correction at a target sky position is obtained as a weighted combination of neighboring HEALPix pixels. The neighborhood is defined by a fixed number of nearest neighboring pixels, which are used as support points for the interpolation. The contribution of neighboring pixels is determined by the RBF kernel as a function of angular distance to ensure locality, such that the interpolation remains locally constrained while preserving global smoothness. The interpolation is formulated to ensure numerical stability through the properties of the RBF kernel. In addition, pixels with insufficient or unreliable estimates are effectively down-weighted or excluded from the reconstruction to avoid unstable local estimates.

The corrections on the \texttt{nside}=256 grid are not directly estimated from observations at that resolution, but are reconstructed from statistically robust coarse-grid estimates, and therefore represent an interpolated continuous field rather than independent measurements. This procedure suppresses sampling noise at small scales while providing a higher-resolution representation suitable for practical use. At the same time, it reduces artificial discontinuities introduced by the original pixelized representation.

The effect of the smoothing procedure is illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:bias_ucac4_zoom_compare_n256}. Compared to the original pixel-based corrections, the reconstructed field exhibits a continuous spatial variation, and the block-like structures at small scales are significantly reduced. At the same time, the large-scale spatial patterns are preserved, indicating that the smoothing primarily reduces noise rather than altering the underlying systematics.

To quantify this improvement, we define an inter-pixel discontinuity metric $\delta_{\mathrm{disc}}$ as the difference in correction values between adjacent HEALPix pixels. The cumulative distributions before and after smoothing are shown in Figure~\ref{fig:bias_jump}. The high-percentile and maximum values of $\delta_{\mathrm{disc}}$ are reduced by factors of roughly 2--4, indicating a substantial improvement in spatial continuity.

\section{Verification} \label{subsec:Verification}

\subsection{Plate Re-reduction Verification}

To independently evaluate the effectiveness of the catalog debiasing model, we compare two reductions of the same Triton CCD frames obtained during 1996--2006. The earlier reduction based on the UCAC2 catalog provides 943 positions \citep{Qiao2007}, while a subsequent re-reduction of the identical images in the Gaia DR2 reference frame yields 1007 positions, including 941 updated measurements \citep{Yan2020}. From these data, 930 strictly corresponding observations are selected, and the Gaia DR2 solution is adopted as the reference.

The total positional separation is defined as
\begin{equation}
\Delta = \sqrt{(\Delta\alpha_*)^2 + (\Delta\delta)^2},
\end{equation}

Before correction, the UCAC2-based solution shows a mean separation of 86.2~mas (median 82.5~mas) relative to the Gaia DR2 re-reduction. After applying the Gaia DR3-based debiasing model, the mean separation decreases to 76.2~mas (median 67.7~mas), corresponding to an improvement of 10.5\%, with 78.0\% of the observations exhibiting reduced residuals.

\begin{figure}[ht!]
\plotone{../Figue/fig_plate_validation.png}
\caption{(a) Cumulative distributions of total positional separation $\Delta$ before (red) and after (blue) debiasing. (b) Temporal evolution of residuals: moving-average trends (upper) and net gain $\Delta_{\mathrm{pre}}-\Delta_{\mathrm{post}}$ (lower).}
\label{fig:plate_validation}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:plate_validation}a shows that the post-debiasing distribution is globally shifted toward smaller separations, indicating a systematic reduction across the full residual range. Figure~\ref{fig:plate_validation}b demonstrates that the improvement persists over the entire time span, with no epoch-dependent degradation.

Although the debiasing model is constructed using Gaia DR3 while the validation reference is based on Gaia DR2, both releases share a consistent ICRS realization, and the DR3--DR2 differences are negligible compared to the original UCAC2 systematic offsets. The convergence toward the Gaia DR2 solution therefore provides a conservative validation of the correction model.

\subsection{Ephemeris Comparison Validation}

As a complementary external test, we compare the observation-minus-ephemeris residuals before and after applying the catalog debiasing corrections. In contrast to the plate re-reduction experiment, which evaluates two reductions of the same image material, the ephemeris comparison probes whether the corrected observations are more consistent with an independent dynamical reference. The emphasis here is therefore placed on the behavior of file-level systematic offsets rather than on a strict reduction of the stochastic scatter.

The validation sample is drawn from natural-satellite astrometric observations available through the IMCCE/NSDC archive, including data reduced with catalogs such as ACT, AGK3, UCAC2, and UCAC4. Observation times are expressed on the TT scale, coordinates are treated in a common ICRS realization, and the external dynamical reference is taken from the JPL Horizons system using the DE441 planetary ephemeris \citep{Park2021DE441}. For this dataset, the statistics are evaluated on paired before/after residuals and summarized at the file level for each reference catalog. We use the mean residuals in RA and Dec to trace residual systematic offsets, while the corresponding standard deviations are used to monitor whether the intrinsic dispersion is preserved. This separation is important because the debiasing procedure is designed primarily to remove catalog-dependent bias terms, not to suppress the random component of the observations.

\section{Conclusions} \label{sec:conclusions}

Using Gaia DR3 as the astrometric reference, we derive systematic position and proper motion corrections for 17 historical star catalogs. A unified catalog debiasing framework is developed based on HEALPix sky partitioning combined with RBF interpolation, enabling the construction of continuous all-sky correction fields while suppressing artificial discontinuities introduced by discrete tiling. The resulting model preserves the intrinsic large-scale structure of catalog systematics while removing non-physical small-scale variations.

Validation based on the plate re-reduction benchmark shows that the debiased data exhibit reduced systematic residuals while maintaining the original dispersion of the observations. This demonstrates that the proposed approach effectively mitigates catalog-dependent systematic offsets without degrading the stochastic error component, leading to improved consistency in orbit determination solutions\citep{Desmars2013}. The improvement is particularly relevant for historical observations that cannot be directly reduced in the Gaia reference frame.

To quantify this improvement, we define an inter-pixel discontinuity metric $\delta_{\mathrm{disc}}$ as the difference in correction values between adjacent HEALPix pixels. The cumulative distributions before and after smoothing are shown in Figure~\ref{fig:bias_jump}. The high-percentile and maximum values of $\delta_{\mathrm{disc}}$ are reduced by factors of roughly 2--4, demonstrating a significant improvement in the spatial continuity of the correction field.

\section{Data and Code Availability} \label{sec:data}

The catalog debiasing tables are provided at two resolutions to support different use cases: a standard grid with \texttt{NSIDE}=64 and a smoothed high-resolution grid with \texttt{NSIDE}=256. Documentation describing the data structure and usage is included with the released products.

The correction tables are publicly available at \url{https://github.com/yangzhe0/nside64} and \url{https://github.com/yangzhe0/nside256}.
